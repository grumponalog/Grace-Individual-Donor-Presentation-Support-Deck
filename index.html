The Constraint
Four things the
building can't do.
  • Receive full semi-truck deliveries safely
  • Accept all available food donations
  • Store the volume of perishable food now available
  • Provide a dignified, choice-based distribution experience
7 / 14
The Stakes
Three truths.
01
Demand grew 25% last year. And it will again.

Nearly 50,000 individuals served in 2025. Without this investment, capacity will continue to fall behind rising demand.

02
The food exists. The infrastructure to capture it doesn't.

Nearly 930 tons (approximately 1.86 million pounds) go to waste in Richland County each year. The problem isn't supply. It's the building's inability to receive, store, and distribute at scale.

03
Food insecurity in childhood isn't something children recover from.

They are in a window of development that doesn't reopen. Meeting that need today creates the stability required for long-term growth tomorrow.

What this investment means for Mansfield
What this
investment does.

Essential infrastructure. Other investments in education, workforce, and health become more effective when food insecurity is removed from the equation.

Reduced strain on every other system. When food insecurity rises, it shows up in hospitals, social services, and schools. A pantry at full capacity absorbs that pressure.

A signal that matters. Where a community directs resources reflects what it values. Grace embodies that choice: Mansfield rebuilt with its most vulnerable residents, not around them.

Funded to last. $1 million of the campaign is designated to cover operations, staffing, and maintenance over time, not just construction. This reduces reliance on year-to-year fundraising and allows the pantry to serve future generations with consistency.
